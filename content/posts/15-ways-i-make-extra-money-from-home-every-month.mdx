Three years ago my extra income from home was zero dollars and one abandoned Etsy shop with two sales, both to my mom. Today it's a real number I track every month, and it's the reason I paid off my credit card without touching my main paycheck.

If you're looking for how to make extra money from home, skip the posts promising you'll replace your job in thirty days. This is the actual list, with real numbers attached, of what has worked for me month over month.

What This List Actually Is

None of this is a get-rich scheme, and I'm not going to pretend any single item on this list changed my life overnight. What changed things was stacking a few of these together and sticking with the ones that actually paid.

15 Ways I Make Extra Money From Home

1. Freelance Writing for Small Business Blogs

I started with two clients found through a Facebook group for local business owners, charging $0.10 a word. That grew into roughly $650 a month within eight months, once I raised my rate and picked up a third client.

2. Selling Printables on Etsy

Budget templates and planner pages, the kind of thing you'd expect from this blog, brought in about $210 a month after the first few months of trial and error on pricing. The upfront work is real, but each listing keeps earning without me touching it again.

3. Virtual Assistant Work for a Handful of Clients

I manage inboxes and scheduling for two small business owners, ten hours a month total, at $25 an hour. It's steady, predictable, and doesn't require me to be creative on days I don't have the energy for it.

4. Reselling Clothes I No Longer Wear

This one isn't recurring in the same way, but a closet clean-out twice a year has netted me $300 to $400 each time on resale apps. It's less a strategy and more a habit I finally stuck with.

5. Proofreading for Grad Students

A friend connected me to a grad student who needed her thesis proofread, and word spread from there. I charge $30 an hour and pick up two to three jobs a month, usually around $180.

6. Tutoring Online Through a Platform

I tutor middle school math twice a week through an online platform that handles the scheduling and payment. It's about $160 a month for two hours a week, and it's the one item on this list I'd do even without the paycheck.

7. Affiliate Links on Existing Blog Content

This isn't a separate hustle so much as making the content I already write work harder. Affiliate income has grown slowly to about $140 a month, mostly from posts about budgeting tools I actually use.

8. Renting Out Storage Space in My Garage

Half my garage sits empty most months, so I list it on a peer-to-peer storage app. It brings in $85 a month for space I wasn't using for anything else.

9. Taking Online Surveys During Downtime

I won't oversell this one. It's genuinely small, around $30 a month, and I only do it while watching TV anyway. It's not a strategy, it's spare change collection, and it's fine as exactly that.

10. Managing Social Media for One Local Business

A local coffee shop pays me a flat $300 a month to handle their Instagram. It's four hours of work spread across the month and doesn't compete with my other freelance clients.

11. Selling Digital Templates Beyond Printables

Beyond the budget printables, I've built a small line of Canva templates for small business owners, which brings in an extra $95 a month with almost no ongoing work required.

12. Pet Sitting for Neighbors

I keep this local and word of mouth only. Two regular clients bring in about $120 a month, and honestly, it's the item on this list that feels the least like work.

13. Renting My Car Out on Weekends I Don't Need It

I work from home most days and my car sits idle. Listing it on a peer-to-peer car rental app brings in $100 to $150 a month depending on demand that particular month.

14. Flipping Furniture Found for Free or Cheap

I check curb finds and marketplace listings for solid furniture that just needs a coat of paint. This is inconsistent, some months zero, other months $200, but it's satisfying enough that I keep doing it.

15. Consulting on Freelance Pricing for New Freelancers

Once I figured out my own pricing, I started offering one-off calls to people just starting out, charging $75 for a 45 minute session. I do maybe two of these a month, so it's small but consistent.

What All of This Adds Up To

Most months, stacking these together puts an extra $1,800 to $2,200 in my account, on top of my main income. Some of that took real upfront effort to build, like the freelance writing client base. Some of it, like the garage storage listing, took an afternoon and now runs itself.

None of it happened by picking one thing and hoping it would replace my job. It happened by testing several, dropping the ones that weren't worth my time, like a dog walking app that paid too little for the driving involved, and doubling down on what actually paid.

If you're figuring out how to make extra money from home, start with one thing you can realistically commit to for three months before judging whether it's working. The stacking effect is real, but it only shows up once you've given more than one thing time to build.
